## Rationale (one short paragraph per pair)

### EN pairs

1. `[reason, will]` — The foundational jurisprudential opposition: Aquinas defines lex as "ordinatio rationis" (an ordinance of reason), against which Austin's command theory positions law as the will of a sovereign backed by sanction. "Reason" carries the Thomistic/Grotian insistence that law is discovered in the rational order of things; "will" compresses the voluntarist-positivist claim that law is posited by an authoritative source.

2. `[morality, legislation]` — Captures the separability thesis head-on: for natural-law theorists (Finnis, Fuller) law is conceptually entangled with morality, while for Hart and Raz the hallmark of a legal system is that its norms derive validity from legislation and social facts, not moral content. The pair isolates the SOURCE dimension cleanly.

3. `[justice, statute]` — "Justice" evokes iustitia as anterior standard (ius naturale); "statute" names the paradigmatic positive-law form. The opposition tracks the classical Ciceronian tension between ius and lex and the common-law distinction between doing justice and applying the statute book.

4. `[conscience, command]` — "Conscience" evokes the Court of Chancery's equitable jurisdiction and the Thomistic synderesis; "command" is the canonical Austinian term. The pair also captures Antigone's dilemma (unwritten laws of conscience vs Creon's decree) which is the archetypal natural/positive confrontation in Western legal imagination.

5. `[nature, enactment]` — Direct lexical mirror of "natural/positive": ius naturale is grounded in nature (rerum natura), ius positivum in deliberate enactment. Finnis's seven basic goods are argued as features of human nature; positivist validity rests on the act of enacting.

6. `[equity, decree]` — Equity (Aristotelian epieikeia, English Chancery) corrects the rigor of positive law by appeal to a higher justice; "decree" here denotes the executive-legislative positive act. Note: "decree" was chosen over "edict" to avoid register drift; the pair isolates the equitable-correction function versus the posited-act function.

7. `[principle, rule]` — Dworkin's celebrated distinction: principles have weight and moral gravity, rules are all-or-nothing outcomes of institutional enactment. The pair captures the natural/positive axis in its most modern Anglo-American articulation (Taking Rights Seriously, 1977) and is neutral with respect to the rights/duties and public/private axes.

8. `[higher law, positive law]` — The explicit terminological opposition inherited from medieval canon law and revived in American constitutional discourse (Corwin, "The Higher Law Background of American Constitutional Law", 1928). Hyphenation-free as a nominal phrase, embedding-friendly. Directly names the axis poles.

9. `[natural justice, procedural edict]` — "Natural justice" is the common-law residue of natural law in judicial review (audi alteram partem, nemo iudex in causa sua), binding even absent statutory source; "procedural edict" names the positive counterpart (codes of civil procedure, practice directions). The pair is specifically calibrated to HK administrative-law vocabulary where natural-justice grounds survive robustly.

10. `[moral order, sovereign fiat]` — Closes the set with the strongest pole-pair: "moral order" (ordo moralis, Grotius's etsi Deus non daretur rational order) versus "sovereign fiat" (Hobbesian-Austinian auctoritas non veritas facit legem). The register matches paragraph-7 Dworkin rhetoric and gives the axis a clear voluntarist-vs-rationalist endpoint.

### ZH pairs

1. `[天理, 国法]` — The canonical triad 天理-国法-人情 (heavenly reason / national law / human sentiment) structures classical Chinese juridical thought from the Tang Code commentaries through Qing magistrate handbooks. 天理 designates the cosmically-grounded moral order, 国法 the enacted law of the realm; together they are the Chinese analogue of the natural/positive opposition. This is the single most doctrinally anchored pair on the ZH side.

2. `[道, 法]` — The Daoist-Legalist opposition: 道 (the Way) is the natural order that requires no enactment; 法 is the hallmark Legalist category (Shang Yang, Han Fei) of posited, publicly promulgated rule. The Han Feizi explicitly defends 法 against 道德 (moral-way reasoning); the pair captures this foundational intra-Chinese debate.

3. `[礼, 令]` — 礼 (the rites, Confucian 礼 as normative order prior to and above positive law, Liji and Analects passim) versus 令 (sovereign edict, imperial order, the administrative-positive form). The pair echoes Xunzi's 隆礼 and contrasts it with the Legalist 令 as sheer command.

4. `[自然, 人为]` — 自然 ("so of itself", the Daoist-naturalist category, Laozi) versus 人为 (artifice, human construction). Maps directly onto Latin natura/artificium and is the purest lexical carrier of the natural/positive axis in vernacular modern Chinese legal-theoretical prose.

5. `[道义, 律令]` — 道义 (moral righteousness, the way-and-righteousness compound invoked in appeals to justice above codes) versus 律令 (statute-and-ordinance, the technical compound for positive-law instruments in the Qin-Han legal system, e.g. 睡虎地秦簡 律令). The pair operates at the same register as the EN "justice/statute" pair.

6. `[良知, 法条]` — 良知 (innate moral knowledge, Wang Yangming's central category in Neo-Confucian moral epistemology) versus 法条 (statutory article, the modern PRC technical term for a numbered provision). The pair imports the Ming-dynasty moral-epistemic tradition against contemporary codified positivism.

7. `[公义, 成文]` — 公义 (public righteousness, justice as shared moral truth) versus 成文 (the written, i.e. written law 成文法). "Written law" as a technical category in Chinese legal theory (成文法 vs 不成文法) tracks the positivist emphasis on enactment-as-textualisation.

8. `[天道, 王法]` — 天道 (the Way of Heaven, cosmic moral order invoked by Mencius and in imperial edicts as the legitimising ground above the ruler) versus 王法 (the king's law, the sovereign's positive command; the phrase 王法无情 "the king's law has no feelings" is proverbial). Captures the pre-modern Chinese natural/positive tension at the highest register.

9. `[义理, 法度]` — 义理 (moral-philosophical principle, the Song Neo-Confucian category for rational-moral order, Zhu Xi) versus 法度 (institutional regulation, the classical compound for established positive-legal measures). The pair tracks the Dworkinian principle/rule distinction in Chinese vocabulary.

10. `[情理, 刑名]` — 情理 (human feeling and reason together, the extra-statutory standard invoked by Qing magistrates to temper the code per the 人情 arm of the classical triad) versus 刑名 (penalty-and-designation, the Legalist technical term for the positive criminal-code apparatus, associated with Shen Buhai and Han Fei). Adds a jurisprudential-philological anchor at the criminal-law end of the axis without invading the rights/duties dimension.

## Known trade-offs and residual ambiguities

**Natural side semantic breadth.** The natural-law pole is unavoidably broader than the positive-law pole because the tradition itself is plural (Thomist, Grotian, Kantian, Fullerian, Dworkinian). I accepted this breadth rather than collapsing all pairs onto, e.g., reason/command alone: Kozlowski axes average ten pair-difference vectors, so a mildly heterogeneous natural cluster is acceptable and probably more robust than a narrow one. The cost is that the axis will partly track "moral-evaluative vocabulary" generally, not only natural-law-specific terminology; this is mitigated by pairing each moral-evaluative term with a distinctively positive-law counter-term (statute, legislation, enactment, decree, sovereign fiat).

**ZH modernisation gap.** Several of the classical ZH pairs (天理/国法, 道/法, 礼/令) live in pre-modern register, while the Sinic models are trained on contemporary Simplified-Chinese corpora including PRC legal texts. Pairs 6, 7, and 9 (良知/法条, 公义/成文, 义理/法度) were deliberately placed in modern-academic register to bridge this gap; the classical pairs remain because they are exactly the ones the thesis needs to detect the 天理/国法 structure predicted by the theoretical framework. A failure-to-detect result would itself be informative: it would indicate that contemporary PRC-trained models have positivistically "forgotten" the classical natural-law axis, which is a finding, not a bug.

**Risk of correlation with `rights_duties` through "自由"-adjacent vocabulary.** Avoided: no pair on either side uses 自由, 权利, 义务, or any of the deontic pair-terms. The natural/positive axis is orthogonal by design: a single provision can be natural-law-grounded yet impose a duty (e.g., the duty not to kill as derivable from practical reason) or positively-enacted yet confer a right (e.g., a statutory right of appeal). The pole-pairs above preserve this orthogonality.
