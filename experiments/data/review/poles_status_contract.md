## Rationale (one short paragraph per pair)

### EN pairs

1. `[status, contract]` — The canonical Mainean dyad from *Ancient Law* (1861), which gives the axis its name. Status designates the position fixed by ascribed social role; contract the obligation generated by consenting wills. Without this pair the axis would not be recognizable as Maine's thesis.

2. `[ascription, agreement]` — The sociological abstraction of Maine's distinction, following Linton and Parsons on ascribed vs achieved roles. "Ascription" captures the mechanism of unchosen allocation of duties, "agreement" the mechanism of consensual generation. This pair isolates the *mechanism* from any particular institutional embodiment.

3. `[birthright, bargain]` — Common-law doublet: "birthright" evokes the primogenitural and droit d'aînesse tradition (and its Blackstonian commentary), "bargain" the consideration-based contractual exchange at the heart of Anglo-American contract doctrine. The alliterative pair is register-consistent and historically loaded.

4. `[kinship, covenant]` — Durkheim's mechanical solidarity vs the contractual covenant in Maine's and Spencer's evolutionary schemes. "Covenant" retains contractual rather than theological valence in legal English (Coke on Littleton; modern deed of covenant). The pair contrasts the binding force of blood with the binding force of a freely given word.

5. `[hierarchy, exchange]` — Dumont (*Homo Hierarchicus*) and Polanyi (*The Great Transformation*) read status orders as hierarchy and contract orders as exchange. Exchange is used here in its transactional, not Maussian-gift, sense, to keep the pair firmly on the contract side.

6. `[lineage, negotiation]` — Lineage fixes position through descent (Maine's "Patria Potestas" passages); negotiation generates position through discursive bargaining (Macneil's relational contract; Atiyah on freedom of contract). The pair tracks the status/contract distinction onto the axis of how a position is *arrived at*.

7. `[caste, party]` — "Caste" is the paradigmatic status category in Weber's sociology of law (closed, ascriptive, endogamous); "party" is the generic legal-subject role in contract doctrine (party to the contract, party of the first part). The pair is deliberately asymmetric in register to encode the asymmetry in how status and contract conceive of the person.

8. `[fealty, stipulation]` — Feudal fealty is the Maine-Bloch archetype of status obligation (*Feudal Society*); "stipulation" is the Roman-civilian term for a formal contractual promise (*stipulatio*) and survives in modern common-law drafting. Both are technical legal terms of comparable register.

9. `[estate, promise]` — "Estate" in the Maitlandian sense designates the legal standing attached to a position in the land-tenure order (estate in land, estate of the realm); "promise" is the atomic unit of contract theory from Fried's *Contract as Promise* to Raz. The pair opposes status-as-standing to contract-as-promise.

10. `[heir, counterparty]` — The heir is defined entirely by succession (non-chosen, non-negotiable); the counterparty is defined entirely by the contractual nexus (chosen, negotiated, dischargeable). The pair reduces the two poles to the minimal form of legal subjecthood each produces.

### ZH pairs

1. `[身份, 契约]` — Direct rendering of Maine's dyad in modern Chinese legal theory. 身份法 vs 契约法 is Xu Xianming's and Jiang Ping's explicit framing of the PRC civil-law modernization narrative (post-1986 General Principles of Civil Law). This pair anchors the axis in Chinese jurisprudential vocabulary.

2. `[名分, 合同]` — 名分 is the Confucian concept of the ascribed role-designation that fixes one's duties (see *Analects* 12.11: «君君臣臣父父子子», "let the ruler be ruler, the subject subject, the father father, the son son"). 合同 is the modern PRC term for contract (Contract Law 1999, now absorbed into the Civil Code 2020 §3). The pair opposes the Confucian ascription of name-and-station to the consensual contract.

3. `[家族, 协议]` — 家族 is the extended family as the primary status unit in classical Chinese law (Qu Tongzu, *Law and Society in Traditional China*); 协议 denotes a negotiated agreement in modern civil practice. Maine's *patria potestas* parallel is explicit in Qu's work.

4. `[宗法, 交换]` — 宗法 (the clan-rule system of the Zhou ritual order, foundational to Chinese family law through the Qing Code) is the densest single word for ascriptive legal order in classical Chinese. 交换 isolates the economic-contractual mechanism of exchange. The pair has no clean EN equivalent and exploits cultural loading as the brief requires.

5. `[世袭, 约定]` — 世袭 (hereditary succession of title, role, office) was the organizing principle of imperial legal status; 约定 is the civilian term for agreed terms (约定条款 in the Civil Code). The pair captures the temporal dimension: status as what is passed down, contract as what is agreed now.

6. `[辈分, 意思表示]` — 辈分 (generational rank within a kinship order) fixes deference and duty by birth position; 意思表示 is the Chinese rendering of *Willenserklärung* (declaration of intent), the technical core of contract formation in the Civil Code (§133 ff.). The pair contrasts traditional rank-by-birth with the modern will-theory of contract.

7. `[宗亲, 缔约]` — 宗亲 (clan kin) designates the agnatic group that classical Chinese law treated as a legal subject for purposes of liability and succession; 缔约 is the act of concluding a contract. The pair opposes belonging-by-descent to binding-by-act.

8. `[门第, 磋商]` — 门第 (family standing, pedigree) is the status marker that governed eligibility for office under the Nine-Rank system and persisted in social practice long after its formal abolition; 磋商 (to negotiate, to confer) is the pre-contractual bargaining stage in modern commercial practice. The pair tracks status/contract onto inherited standing vs negotiated terms.

9. `[族规, 订立]` — 族规 (clan regulations, the unwritten and semi-written customary rules governing a lineage) is the paradigmatic non-state status-based normative order in late-imperial and Republican China. 订立 (to conclude, to draw up) is the formal verb for contract formation. The pair opposes rule-by-ascription to rule-by-drafting.

10. `[血缘, 买卖]` — 血缘 (blood tie, consanguinity) is the ultimate ascriptive principle in Chinese family law and inheritance; 买卖 (sale, purchase) is the most basic contractual transaction and the paradigm of free exchange. The pair reduces both poles to their minimal form: the unchosen tie vs the chosen trade.

## Known trade-offs and residual ambiguities

The sharpest trade-off is the tension between *historicist* clarity and *present-tense* legal salience. Maine framed the status/contract movement as a diachronic law of progressive societies, which means several of the clearest pole words (fealty, estate, 宗法, 世袭) sit in historical rather than live-doctrinal vocabulary. I accepted this cost because the axis is precisely a historicist instrument: it is supposed to measure whether the embedding geometry of modern legal language still carries the sediment of the status-to-contract transition. Pairs 3, 4, 6, 7, 10 on both sides lean modern-live; pairs 4, 8, 9 on both sides lean classical-sedimentary; the mix is deliberate.

A second, sharper ambiguity concerns overlap with `individual_collective` at the Chinese pole 家族 (pair EN-4 conceptual vicinity; ZH-3). 家族 is both a collectivity (individual_collective sense) and an ascription mechanism (status_contract sense). I kept it here because the *opposition* with 协议 pins the sense firmly to the mechanism-of-binding reading rather than the I-vs-we reading: the contrast is "family-as-given" versus "agreement-as-made", not "group" versus "individual". Embedding geometry should pick up the contrastive axis rather than either pole in isolation, which is why Kozlowski averaging over ten pairs is robust to a single ambiguous word. The same reasoning applies to `[caste, party]` on the EN side, where "caste" could drift into the individual_collective orbit; the counterpart "party" pulls the whole pair back onto the mechanism axis.

- `[家族, 协议]` ZH: see the paragraph above on residual ambiguity. Retained because the contrast word 协议 disambiguates.
- `[estate, promise]` EN: "estate" has a thin connection to public_private's "institutional/domestic"; kept because "promise" forces the contract-theoretic reading.

No pair is repeated in either polarity direction across the four axes.
