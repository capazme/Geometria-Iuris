§3.1.3c Neighborhood Trajectory Analysis — 101 candidate terms across 6 models — k=7 nearest neighbors

What is this table?

Each of the 101 candidate terms was run through the Neighborhood Trajectory Analysis (NTA) on all 6 models. For each term, we track its $k=7$ nearest neighbors across sampled transformer layers (L0, L4, L8, ..., L24). The neighbor pool includes 397 core legal terms and 100 control terms (Swadesh-like everyday words).

The columns measure different aspects of how a term's neighborhood evolves with depth:

  • Peak Ctrl — Maximum number of control (non-legal) neighbors at any layer, averaged across 6 models. High values mean the term starts in "general-purpose" semantic space.
  • Final Ctrl — Control neighbors remaining at the final layer. $>0$ means the model never fully resolves the term into legal space (persistent polysemy).
  • Volatility — Total absolute change in ctrl count across layer transitions $\sum_\ell |\text{ctrl}_{\ell+1} - \text{ctrl}_\ell|$. High = turbulent trajectory.
  • Dom Final — Number of distinct legal domains among the final-layer neighbors. High = term sits at a cross-domain intersection.
  • Own Dom — How many of the final $k$ neighbors share the term's own domain. High = the model has strongly categorized it within its domain.
  • Crystal — Average layer at which ctrl drops to 0 permanently ("crystallization"). Earlier = the term is recognized as legal sooner. "—" = never fully crystallizes.
$$\text{Score} = 2 \cdot \overline{\text{PeakCtrl}} + \overline{\text{Volatility}} + 3 \cdot \overline{\text{FinalCtrl}} + \overline{\text{DomFinal}}$$

Click any row to see that term's full trajectory in the Trajectories tab.

Reading the trajectory charts

Each chart shows one model's view of the selected term. The x-axis is the transformer layer (sampled at L0, L4, L8, ...). The y-axis counts how many of the $k=7$ nearest neighbors are legal (colored area) vs control/non-legal (gray area). The two areas always sum to $k=7$.

A term that starts with many control neighbors and ends with all legal neighbors demonstrates the model's process of semantic specialization: surface layers see the term as general-purpose, deeper layers recognize its legal specificity.

The combined chart at the bottom overlays all 6 models' control curves. Convergence across models suggests a robust pattern; divergence suggests cultural or architectural differences in how models encode the term.

Control-neighbor heatmap

Each cell shows the average number of control neighbors (across all 6 models) for a given term at a given layer. Terms are sorted by their interest score (most interesting at top).

Reading the pattern: A column of dark cells at L0 that fades rightward means most terms start with many control neighbors and shed them as depth increases. Terms that remain dark across all layers are persistently polysemic — the models never fully resolve them as legal-specific. These are often the most interesting for the thesis.

Domain-level patterns

The top chart shows the average control-neighbor trajectory per domain, aggregated across all terms in that domain and all 6 models. This reveals which legal domains are recognized earliest (ctrl drops fast) and which remain entangled with everyday language longest (ctrl stays high).

The bottom chart shows a bar comparison of mean interest score by domain. Domains with higher average scores contain terms that are, on average, more polysemic and more interesting for the thesis.

Crystallization layer distribution

At which layer does each term lose its last control neighbor permanently? Terms that never crystallize ("Never") retain non-legal neighbors even at the deepest layer.

Cross-tradition divergence

This tab compares how WEIRD (BGE-EN, E5, FreeLaw) and Sinic (BGE-ZH, Text2vec, Dmeta) model families treat the same legal concept. Recall that Sinic models receive the Chinese translation of each term.

The divergence score combines two differences:

$$\text{Div} = |\overline{\text{OwnDom}}_W - \overline{\text{OwnDom}}_S| + 2 \cdot |\overline{\text{FinalCtrl}}_W - \overline{\text{FinalCtrl}}_S|$$

High divergence means the two cultural-linguistic traditions encode the concept in structurally different semantic neighborhoods. This is directly relevant to the thesis claim about cultural normative structures.

Left scatter: Each point is a term. X = WEIRD final ctrl, Y = Sinic final ctrl. Points on the diagonal are treated similarly by both traditions. Points far from the diagonal show cross-cultural encoding differences.
