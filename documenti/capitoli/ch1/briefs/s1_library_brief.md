## EN_DigiVolksgeist_Puzio.pdf

**Full citation**: Puzio, G., "Emergent values from Large Language Models: the digital crystallisation of the Volksgeist," Diritto ed Etica delle Tecnologie Emergenti, LUISS, a.a. 2025/2026.
**Pages read**: 1-10
**Relevance to S1.1**: This is the user's own prior work. It contains the Galileo telescope analogy, the bias-as-grammar-of-learning argument, the Volksgeist-LLM isomorphism, and a discussion of Kelsen's Reine Rechtslehre as foil. Highly relevant as direct intellectual precedent for S1.1.

### Extract 1 -- Telescope analogy
- Quote: "In 1610, Galileo pointed his telescope towards Jupiter and saw what no human eye had ever seen: four moons in orbit around the planet. The instrument had not created those moons, it had rendered them observable. It had transformed the hypothesis that celestial bodies could orbit around centres other than the Earth into an empirical fact." (p. 8)
- Context: Section 4.3 "The jurist's telescope." Puzio argues that LLMs offer jurists an analogous opportunity: for the first time, the Volksgeist becomes empirically probeable.
- Use in S1.1: This analogy can be repurposed for the thesis's framing -- legal science has lacked its telescope; embedding models may constitute a first approximation of one.

### Extract 2 -- LLMs as methodological instruments
- Quote: "Large Language Models offer the jurist and the sociologist of law an analogous opportunity. The *Volksgeist*, for two centuries, has been a suggestive philosophical concept, fecund, but indemonstrable. How does one measure the spirit of a people? How does one verify that law emerges from collective consciousness and not from the will of the legislator? The Historical School could only argue by analogy, by historical reconstruction, by hermeneutical intuition." (p. 8)
- Context: The paper argues that LLMs make it possible to measure cultural values with statistical precision.
- Use in S1.1: Establishes the "epistemological gap" -- legal science had concepts (Volksgeist, living law) but no measurement instrument.

### Extract 3 -- LLM as unprecedented instrument
- Quote: "The LLM thus emerges as a methodological instrument of unprecedented power. The weights of the model *are* the *Weltansicht* of a culture rendered legible; the biases *are* its normative inclinations rendered measurable; the emergent behaviour *is* its *lebendes Recht* rendered interrogable." (p. 8)
- Context: Conclusion of the telescope section. The passage compresses the entire argument.
- Use in S1.1: Can be cited as prior formulation of the thesis's core claim -- that embedding models can serve as instruments for measuring legal meaning.

### Extract 4 -- Bias as grammar of learning
- Quote: "The point is decisive: bias is not a defect to be corrected, but the deep grammar of machine learning. Just as there is no language without grammar and without rules that constrain possible combinations, there is no learning without assumptions that orient the search for patterns." (pp. 4-5)
- Context: Section 3.1 "Bias as the grammar of learning." Distinguishes statistical bias from inductive bias (citing Mitchell 1980).
- Use in S1.1: Supports the argument that bias in models is structurally informative, not merely noise -- foundational for treating embeddings as cultural instruments.

### Extract 5 -- Kelsen's Reine Rechtslehre as foil
- Quote: "Hans Kelsen's *Reine Rechtslehre* (1960) offers a radically different model of legal validity. For Kelsen, the validity of a norm cannot be derived from facts, from social practices, customs, or collective consciousness. This would commit the naturalistic fallacy of deriving an *Ought* from an *Is*." (p. 7, fn. 35: KELSEN, H., *Reine Rechtslehre*, 2. Aufl., Franz Deuticke, Wien 1960.)
- Context: Section 4.1 contrasts Savigny/Ehrlich (law from below) with Kelsen (law from above). The empirical evidence from LLMs vindicates the former.
- Use in S1.1: Kelsen represents the tradition that denies empirical access to legal meaning. The thesis can position its approach against this background.

### Extract 6 -- Cultural specificity of models
- Quote: "The correlation between the similarity of LLM-human responses and the cultural distance from the United States is strongly negative (r = -0.70): the more culturally distant a population is from the Anglophone-Western world, the less its response patterns coincide with those of the models." (p. 5, citing Atari et al. 2023)
- Context: Section 3.2 "Empirical evidence of cultural specificity."
- Use in S1.1: Empirical evidence that models are culturally specific instruments, not universal mirrors -- relevant to the epistemological limits of computational approaches.

### Extract 7 -- Epistemological inversion
- Quote: "For two centuries, the *Volksgeist* has been an explanatory concept: a hypothetical cause behind observable effects. Large Language Models invert this relationship. The algorithmic *Volksgeist* is not that which explains the values of the model: it is that which the values of the model render visible. It is no longer cause, but object; no longer hypothesis, but datum." (p. 9)
- Context: Section 5, Conclusions. This is the paper's core thesis.
- Use in S1.1: The epistemological inversion from speculation to observation is exactly the gap that S1.1 needs to articulate.

## Summary: Key themes for S1.1

### 1. The epistemological gap
- Legal science is a science of meaning (Choi, Bhupatiraju, Hildebrandt/COHUBICOL) that has lacked instruments for measuring meaning (Puzio, Lettieri, Bhupatiraju).
- The gap is between *conception* (case-space models, Volksgeist, living law) and *measurement* (no legal equivalent of the telescope).

### 2. Computational approaches so far: instrumental, not epistemological
- CLS (Chau & Livermore) and legal NLP (Bhupatiraju) use computation to predict, classify, and automate -- they *consume* legal meaning without *examining* it.
- Even "measurement" papers like Choi (2024) measure single properties (clarity) rather than the full geometry of meaning.
- Lettieri (2018) frames computation as enabling new platforms and methods, but the epistemological question (what do these methods reveal about the nature of legal meaning?) is never asked.

### 3. The missing step: embeddings as scientific instruments
- Aceves & Evans (2023) provide the clearest methodological precedent: embeddings represent the conceptual space of a social system. This framework has not yet been applied to legal science in an epistemological mode.
- Puzio (2025/2026) formulated the thesis's intellectual antecedent: the telescope analogy, the bias-as-grammar argument, the Volksgeist-LLM isomorphism.
- Hildebrandt/COHUBICOL provides the philosophical framework: law's mode of existence is contingent on its information infrastructure; computation may transform it.

### 4. The Wittgensteinian foundation
- Choi (2024) explicitly grounds computational analysis of legal text in the use theory of meaning.
- Puzio (2025/2026) develops the Wittgenstein-Lebensform connection.
- This is the philosophical bridge: distributional semantics (you shall know a word by the company it keeps) operationalizes Wittgenstein's use theory. Embeddings measure meaning-as-use, not meaning-as-reference.
