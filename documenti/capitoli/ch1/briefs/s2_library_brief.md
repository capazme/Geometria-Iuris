## 1. DigiVolksgeist (Puzio 2025) — Jurisprudential tradition

### 1.1 Savigny: law as emergent from collective consciousness

> «Das Recht hat seine Wurzel in dem gemeinsamen Bewußtseyn des Volkes»
> ("law has its root in the common consciousness of the people")

- **Location**: p. 2, fn. 9
- **Original source**: SAVIGNY, F. C. VON, *System des heutigen römischen Rechts*, Bd. I, Veit, Berlin 1840, §7.

> «aus der Betrachtung des Volkes, in dessen Rechtsbewußtseyn das Recht selbst seine Wurzel hat»

- **Location**: p. 6, fn. 34
- **Original source**: SAVIGNY, F. C. VON, *System des heutigen römischen Rechts*, Bd. VIII, Veit, Berlin 1849, §400.

**Law-language analogy** (paraphrase from DigiVolksgeist p. 2):
Savigny's constitutive analogy is with language. No one invents a grammar; no one decrees the rules of syntax. Yet grammar exists, binds, and is transmitted from generation to generation. In the same manner, customary law is not deliberated: it precipitates slowly from daily practices, from repeated contracts, from consolidated expectations. Jurists do not create this law; they articulate it, bring it to consciousness, systematise it, render it technically operative. But the raw material, the spirit that informs the rules, pre-exists their intervention.

**Use in §1.2**: Establishes the foundational analogy (law ≈ language in mode of emergence). The claim is epistemological: legal meaning is a collective phenomenon arising from practices, not decreed by individual actors. This sets up the later bridge to the distributional hypothesis.

### 1.2 Herder: language as natural organ, Volksgeist

- **Location**: pp. 2-3, fnn. 11-12
- **Original source**: HERDER, J. G., *Abhandlung über den Ursprung der Sprache*, Voss, Berlin 1772.

Herder argued that language is not an instrument external to thought, but its "natural organ." Every people possesses a peculiar spirit, a *Volksgeist*, that expresses itself above all in its language. Language does not mirror the world; it articulates it according to categories proper to that community and to no other.

**Use in §1.2**: Herder provides the philosophical root for the claim that language is constitutive (not merely representative). Connects to Humboldt's radicalisation of the same insight.

---

### 1.3 Humboldt: Weltansicht, language as formative organ of thought

> «Die Sprache ist das bildende Organ des Gedankens»
> ("language is the formative organ of thought")

- **Location**: p. 3, fnn. 13-15
- **Original source**: HUMBOLDT, W. VON, *Über die Verschiedenheit des menschlichen Sprachbaues und ihren Einfluss auf die geistige Entwicklung des Menschengeschlechts*, Dümmler, Berlin 1836, §9.

Every language is a *Weltansicht* (worldview), a peculiar way of segmenting experience. Language does not translate pre-existing thoughts into words; it generates the thoughts themselves.

**Implication drawn in DigiVolksgeist** (p. 3): If law is analogous to language in its emergence (Savigny), and if language constitutes rather than represents the conceptual world of its speakers (Humboldt), then every legal tradition does not merely *describe* a normative reality — it *constitutes* one. The legal vocabulary of a tradition is not a neutral labelling system; it is the very medium through which normative objects acquire their determinate shape.

**Use in §1.2**: The Savigny-Humboldt synthesis is the core philosophical move. It transforms the law-language analogy from a metaphor into an epistemological claim about legal meaning constitution. This is the claim that the distributional hypothesis will later operationalise.

### 1.4 Wittgenstein: meaning as use, Lebensform, rule-following

#### Lebensform and Sprachspiel

> «Und eine Sprache vorstellen heisst, sich eine Lebensform vorstellen»
> ("And to imagine a language means to imagine a form of life")

- **Location**: p. 3, fn. 17
- **Original source**: WITTGENSTEIN, L., *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, Blackwell, Oxford 1953, §19.

> «das Sprechen der Sprache ein Teil ist einer Tätigkeit, oder einer Lebensform»
> ("the speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life")

- **Location**: p. 3, fn. 18
- **Original source**: *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, §23.

#### Rule-following as practice

> «Einer Regel folgen, eine Mitteilung machen, einen Befehl geben, eine Schachpartie spielen sind Gepflogenheiten (Gebräuche, Institutionen)»
> ("To obey a rule, to make a report, to give an order, to play a game of chess, are customs, uses, institutions")

- **Location**: p. 3, fn. 19
- **Original source**: *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, §199.

Also §202: «Darum ist 'der Regel folgen' eine Praxis. Und der Regel zu folgen glauben ist nicht: der Regel folgen. Und darum kann man nicht der Regel 'privatim' folgen.»

#### Agreement in form of life

> «Richtig und falsch ist, was Menschen sagen, und in der Sprache stimmen die Menschen überein. Dies ist keine Übereinstimmung der Meinungen, sondern der Lebensform.»
> ("What is true or false is what human beings say, and they agree in the language they use. This is not agreement in opinions but in form of life.")

- **Location**: p. 3, fn. 20
- **Original source**: *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, §241.

**Use in §1.2**: Wittgenstein is the philosophical hinge of the entire section. "Meaning is use" transforms the Savigny-Humboldt tradition into a thesis amenable to empirical operationalisation. If meaning is use, and use is observable shared practice, then meaning is *in principle* measurable. The transition from Wittgenstein to the distributional hypothesis is the central argumentative move of §1.2.

### 1.5 Ehrlich: lebendes Recht vs. Entscheidungsnormen

- **Location**: p. 4, fnn. 21-22
- **Original source**: EHRLICH, E., *Grundlegung der Soziologie des Rechts*, Duncker & Humblot, München-Leipzig 1913, spec. ch. II-III.

Ehrlich distinguished between *Entscheidungsnormen* ("norms of decision" — what courts apply) and *lebendes Recht* ("living law" — the norms that actually govern social life). The contract concluded with a handshake, reciprocal expectations in neighbourly relations, professional customs no code has sanctioned: all this is living law.

**Key methodological point**: If there exists a gap between declared norm and practised norm, then the meaning of legal concepts as they function in social life may differ systematically from their meaning as defined in statute books. Ehrlich's method for investigating this gap was sociological (observation, interviews, study of contractual practices) — empirical, but qualitative and local. He could study the living law of Bukovina; he could not measure the semantic structure of an entire legal vocabulary or compare it systematically across traditions. The aspiration was there; the instrument was not.

**Use in §1.2**: Ehrlich closes the jurisprudential arc. He makes the empirical aspiration explicit but reveals the methodological gap: qualitative observation cannot scale. This sets up the need for the distributional hypothesis as the missing operationalisation.

## 2. Sahlgren (2008) — The Distributional Hypothesis

**Full ref**: SAHLGREN, M., "The Distributional Hypothesis," *Italian Journal of Linguistics*, 20(1), 2008, pp. 33-53.

### 2.1 Saussure: valeur and structural meaning

> "In the language itself, there are only differences"

- **Location**: p. 38 (p. 6 of PDF)
- **Original source**: SAUSSURE, F. DE, *Cours de linguistique générale*, 1916; English trans. Harris 1983, pp. 166/118.

Sahlgren traces the distributional hypothesis to Saussurean structuralism. The concept of *valeur* (value) is key: a linguistic sign's value is determined not by any intrinsic property but by its relations to other signs in the system. "The value of just any term is accordingly determined by its environment" (Saussure, cited Sahlgren p. 38).

Sahlgren distinguishes two types of structural relations from Saussure:
- **Syntagmatic relations**: relations between elements that co-occur in a sequence (in praesentia)
- **Paradigmatic relations**: relations between elements that can substitute for each other (in absentia)

**Use in §1.2**: Saussure provides the structural-linguistic foundation for the distributional hypothesis. The concept of *valeur* — meaning as determined by relations, not by reference — is the linguistic counterpart of Wittgenstein's "meaning is use." §1.2 needs to establish this parallel.

### 2.2 Harris: distributional correlate of meaning

> "difference of meaning correlates with difference of distribution"

- **Location**: p. 36 (p. 4 of PDF)
- **Original source**: HARRIS, Z. S., *Papers in Structural Linguistics*, Reidel, Dordrecht 1970, p. 786.

Harris excluded meaning from the explanans of linguistic analysis but acknowledged that distributional patterns correlate with meaning differences:

> "It may still be 'due to meaning' in one sense, but it accords with a distributional regularity"

- **Location**: pp. 35-36 (pp. 3-4 of PDF)
- **Original source**: HARRIS, Z. S., 1970, p. 785.

Sahlgren notes that Harris's position is subtler than often recognised: he did not claim that distribution *is* meaning, but that distributional differences *correlate with* meaning differences. The distributional hypothesis is therefore a *correlation claim*, not an identity claim.

**Use in §1.2**: Harris provides the formal bridge between the philosophical tradition (meaning is use/practice) and the computational procedure (meaning can be approximated by distributional patterns). The correlation claim is methodologically crucial: it justifies using distributional data as *evidence* for meaning without reducing meaning to distribution.

### 2.3 The refined distributional hypothesis

> "The refined distributional hypothesis: A distributional model accumulated from co-occurrence information contains syntagmatic relations between words, while a distributional model accumulated from shared neighbors contains paradigmatic relations between words."

- **Location**: p. 40 (p. 8 of PDF)

This is Sahlgren's own contribution: different computational implementations of the distributional hypothesis capture different types of semantic relations. Co-occurrence models (word-context matrices) capture syntagmatic relations; models based on shared distributional neighbours capture paradigmatic relations.

**Use in §1.2**: The refined hypothesis matters for the thesis because embedding models (which use contextual prediction, a form of co-occurrence) capture primarily syntagmatic/contextual relations. This has implications for what the experiments can and cannot measure.

## 3. Firth (1957) — Contextual Theory of Meaning

**Full ref**: FIRTH, J. R., "A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory, 1930-1955," in *Studies in Linguistic Analysis*, Special Volume of the Philological Society, Blackwell, Oxford 1957 (repr. 1962).

### 3.1 The canonical quote

> "You shall know a word by the company it keeps!"

- **Location**: p. 11 (Section IV of the essay)

This is the most frequently cited formulation of the distributional hypothesis in computational linguistics, though Firth's framework is broader than the purely distributional.

### 3.2 Meaning as complex of contextual relations

> "Meaning, that is to say, is to be regarded as a complex of contextual relations, and phonetics, grammar, lexicography, and semantics each handles its own components of the complex in its appropriate context."

- **Location**: p. 6

Firth's theory disperses meaning across multiple "levels of analysis" — phonological, grammatical, collocational, situational. No single level exhausts meaning. This is the doctrine of "meaning by collocation" at the lexical level.

### 3.3 Words as observable social acts

> "By regarding words as acts, events, habits, we limit our inquiry to what is objective and observable in the group life of our fellows."

- **Location**: p. 2

This aligns Firth with the Wittgensteinian position: meaning is observable in practices, not hidden in mental states.

### 3.4 Explicit reference to Wittgenstein

> "the meaning of words lies in their use"

- **Location**: p. 11
- Firth explicitly cites Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* here.

### 3.5 Collocation

> "One of the meanings of *night* is its collocability with *dark*, and of *dark*, of course, collocation with *night*."

- **Location**: p. 11

Collocation is Firth's specific mechanism for how meaning is constituted by co-occurrence at the lexical level.

**Use in §1.2**: Firth is the bridge figure between philosophy (Wittgenstein) and computational linguistics (distributional models). His "company it keeps" formulation is the direct ancestor of the distributional hypothesis as implemented in embedding models. The fact that Firth explicitly cites Wittgenstein strengthens the argumentative chain from philosophical insight to empirical procedure.

## 4. Harris (1954) — Distributional Structure

**Full ref**: HARRIS, Z. S., "Distributional Structure," *WORD*, 10(2-3), 1954, pp. 146-162.

### 4.1 Core definitions

> "each language can be described in terms of a distributional structure, i.e. in terms of the occurrence of parts (or features) relative to other parts"

- **Location**: p. 146

> "The distribution of an element will be understood as the sum of all its environments"

- **Location**: p. 146

> Distribution = "the total of all different environments of [an element], i.e. the total of all the different existing array of co-occurrents of [that element]"

- **Location**: p. 146

### 4.2 Structure as real pattern in data

> "the scientific structure states a network of relations, and these relations really hold in the data investigated"

- **Location**: p. 149

This is epistemologically important: Harris claims that distributional structure is not an artefact of the analyst's method but a real pattern in the data. The structure discovered by distributional analysis is objective.

### 4.3 Distributional structure in speakers

Harris notes that distributional regularities are reflected in the speaking habits of the community:

> the distributional structure is present not only in the corpus but in the speakers themselves, as patterns of expectation and production.

- **Location**: p. 150 (paraphrase — the passage discusses how distributional facts are "known" to speakers through their habits)

**Use in §1.2**: Harris 1954 is the formal origin of the distributional methodology. The definitions of distribution and environment are the technical foundations that embedding models later implement computationally. The claim that structure "really holds in the data" grounds the epistemological argument: distributional analysis discovers real patterns, it does not impose them.

## Argument Map for §1.2

The sources support the following argumentative chain:

```
PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATION                    LINGUISTIC OPERATIONALISATION
─────────────────────────                   ─────────────────────────────
Savigny: law ≈ language                     Saussure: valeur = meaning by relations
  (emergence from collective practice)        (Sahlgren 2008, p.38)
         │                                           │
Herder/Humboldt: language constitutes        Harris: distribution correlates with meaning
  (not represents) conceptual world            (Harris 1954; Sahlgren 2008, p.36)
         │                                           │
Wittgenstein: meaning = use                  Firth: "company it keeps"
  (observable in shared practice)              (Firth 1957, p.11 — cites Wittgenstein)
         │                                           │
Ehrlich: living law ≠ declared law           Distributional hypothesis:
  (empirical aspiration, qualitative limit)    meaning ≈ distributional pattern
         │                                           │
         └──────────── BRIDGE ──────────────────────┘
                        │
              Embedding models as instruments
              that operationalise "meaning is use"
              at scale (→ §1.3, §2.3)
```

### Key themes for §1.2 writer

1. **The Savigny-Humboldt-Wittgenstein arc**: Law emerges like language (Savigny) → language constitutes thought (Humboldt) → meaning is use in shared practice (Wittgenstein). Each step deepens the claim.

2. **Wittgenstein as hinge**: The transition from "meaning is use" to "use can be measured" is the central argumentative move. Firth's explicit citation of Wittgenstein (p. 11) provides textual evidence that the linguistic tradition was aware of the philosophical connection.

3. **Harris's correlation claim**: Distribution is not meaning, but distributional differences correlate with meaning differences. This is methodologically crucial — it justifies the empirical procedure without committing to reductionism.

4. **Ehrlich's gap**: The aspiration to observe legal meaning empirically existed in the jurisprudential tradition; the instrument did not. The distributional hypothesis provides the missing instrument.

5. **Sahlgren's refinement**: Different distributional models capture different semantic relations (syntagmatic vs. paradigmatic). This matters for interpreting what embedding experiments measure.
