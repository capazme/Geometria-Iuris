## 1. SAVIGNY — Additional material beyond DigiVolksgeist

### Already covered in DigiVolksgeist (do not duplicate):

### Quote 1 — Law, language, custom as organically linked [UNVERIFIED — exact wording needs physical verification]
- Concept: In §7 of the *System*, Savigny argues that law, language, and custom (*Recht, Sprache, Sitte*) do not have a separate existence (*kein abgesondertes Daseyn*) but are organically interconnected manifestations of the same Volksgeist. They are "innerlich verbundene Krafte" (internally connected forces).
- Location: *System des heutigen romischen Rechts*, Bd. I, Veit, Berlin 1840, §7, pp. 14-15.
- Source of verification: The concept is universally attested in secondary literature (Britannica, Encyclopedia.com, Wikipedia "Friedrich Carl von Savigny", Springer Reference). The exact German phrasing needs verification against the Deutsches Textarchiv digital edition (available at deutschestextarchiv.de/book/show/savigny_system01_1840).

### Quote 2 — The grammar analogy from *Vom Beruf* [VERIFIED]
- Concept: In *Vom Beruf unsrer Zeit*, Savigny uses the analogy between law and language explicitly: just as no one invents a grammar and no one decrees the rules of syntax, yet grammar exists, binds, and is transmitted from generation to generation, so customary law precipitates from practice without legislative deliberation.
- Translation from the 1831 Hayward English edition: "by internal, silently operating powers, not by the arbitrary will of a lawgiver."
- Location: *Vom Beruf unsrer Zeit*, Heidelberg 1814. English transl.: *Of the Vocation of Our Age for Legislation and Jurisprudence*, transl. A. Hayward, Littlewood, London 1831.
- Source of verification: Confirmed in Britannica, Encyclopedia.com, and Google Books (Hayward translation available in full). The narrative in DigiVolksgeist (p. 2) paraphrases this passage without quoting the German directly.

### Quote 3 — Savigny's four elements of interpretation [VERIFIED]
- Already documented in s1_research_brief.md under Larenz. For section 1.2, the relevant point is that Savigny's interpretive method — grammatical, logical, historical, systematic — is itself modeled on philological method: the jurist approaches legal texts with the same hermeneutic tools the philologist uses for literary texts.
- Location: *System*, Bd. I, pp. 213-214, §33.
- Source of verification: Confirmed in s1 brief; cross-reference with Larenz, *Methodenlehre*, 6th ed. (1991).

## 2. WITTGENSTEIN — Exact German for §43 and §23

### Already covered in DigiVolksgeist:
- §19: "Und eine Sprache vorstellen heisst, sich eine Lebensform vorstellen"
- §23: "Das Wort 'Sprachspiel' soll hier hervorheben, dass das Sprechen der Sprache ein Teil ist einer Tatigkeit, oder einer Lebensform"
- §199: "Einer Regel folgen, eine Mitteilung machen, einen Befehl geben, eine Schachpartie spielen sind Gepflogenheiten (Gebrauche, Institutionen)"
- §202: "Darum ist 'der Regel folgen' eine Praxis"
- §241: "Dies ist keine Ubereinstimmung der Meinungen, sondern der Lebensform"

### Quote 1 — §43: Meaning as use [VERIFIED]
- Original: "Man kann fur eine grosse Klasse von Fallen der Benutzung des Wortes 'Bedeutung' -- wenn auch nicht fur alle Falle seiner Benutzung -- dieses Wort so erklaren: Die Bedeutung eines Wortes ist sein Gebrauch in der Sprache."
- Translation: "For a large class of cases of the use of the word 'meaning' -- though not for all cases of its use -- this word can be explained thus: the meaning of a word is its use in the language."
- Location: L. WITTGENSTEIN, *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, Blackwell, Oxford 1953, §43. Standard edition: *Philosophical Investigations*, 4th ed., ed. P.M.S. Hacker and J. Schulte, Wiley-Blackwell, Oxford 2009.
- Source of verification: The Ludwig Wittgenstein Project (wittgensteinproject.org) provides the full German text. The English translation is from the Anscombe/Hacker/Schulte edition. Confirmed across Wikipedia, Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, and multiple academic sources. The qualification "if not for all" is crucial — Wittgenstein does not make an absolutist claim.

### Quote 2 — §23: Language-game as activity / Lebensform [VERIFIED]
- Original: "Das Wort 'Sprachspiel' soll hier hervorheben, dass das Sprechen der Sprache ein Teil ist einer Tatigkeit, oder einer Lebensform."
- Translation: "The word 'language-game' is meant to bring into prominence the fact that the speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life."
- Location: *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, §23.
- Source of verification: Wittgenstein Project (wittgensteinproject.org), Wikipedia ("Philosophische Untersuchungen"), confirmed verbatim.

### Quote 3 — §23: List of language-games [VERIFIED]
- Original: Wittgenstein provides a list of language-games including "Befehlen, und nach Befehlen handeln — [...] Bitten, Danken, Fluchen, Grussen, Beten."
- Translation: "Giving orders, and obeying them — [...] Requesting, thanking, cursing, greeting, praying."
- Location: *Philosophische Untersuchungen*, §23 (the list immediately precedes the Lebensform quote).
- Source of verification: Wittgenstein Project, confirmed in getabstract.com summary.

### Supplementary: §43 relevance to §1.2
- The §43 formulation is NOT quoted in DigiVolksgeist, which focuses on §19, §23, §199, §202, §241. Section 1.2 can use §43 as the theoretical pivot: if meaning is use, then meaning is constituted by patterns of practice — and patterns of practice are, in principle, observable. This bridges the hermeneutic tradition (meaning as interpretation) to the empirical possibility (meaning as measurable structure).

## 3. SAUSSURE — *Cours de linguistique generale* (1916)

**Bibliographic reference**: SAUSSURE, F. de, *Cours de linguistique generale*, ed. Ch. Bally and A. Sechehaye, with A. Riedlinger, Payot, Lausanne-Paris 1916, 336 pp. Critical edition: ed. T. De Mauro, Payot, Paris 1967 (reprinted 2005, ISBN 978-2-228-88942-1). English translation: *Course in General Linguistics*, transl. W. Baskin, Philosophical Library, New York 1959; revised ed. by P. Meisel and H. Saussy, Columbia University Press, New York 2011.

**Relevance to §1.2**: Saussure provides the structuralist bridge between the Romantic tradition (Herder, Humboldt, Savigny) and the later Wittgensteinian pragmatic turn. His distinction between *langue* (the abstract social system of signs) and *parole* (individual speech acts) formalizes the insight that language is simultaneously a social structure and an individual practice. For §1.2, Saussure is the missing link: he shows that language (and by analogy, law) can be studied as a *system* with internal regularities — an insight that makes computational analysis conceivable.

### Quote 1 — Langue as a system of signs [VERIFIED]
- Original: "La langue est un systeme de signes exprimant des idees, et par la, comparable a l'ecriture, a l'alphabet des sourds-muets, aux rites symboliques, aux formes de politesse, aux signaux militaires, etc., etc."
- Translation: "Language is a system of signs expressing ideas, and is therefore comparable to writing, the sign-language of deaf-mutes, symbolic rites, forms of politeness, military signals, etc."
- Location: *Cours de linguistique generale*, Part I, Ch. III ("Object of Linguistics"), p. 33 of the 1916 Payot edition (p. 34 in the 1971 De Mauro reprint).
- Source of verification: Wikisource full text (fr.wikisource.org/wiki/Cours_de_linguistique_generale/Texte_entier), confirmed page reference via Wikisource djvu page 34.

### Quote 2 — The langue/parole distinction [UNVERIFIED — page number approximate]
- Concept: Saussure distinguishes *langue* (the social, systematic, homogeneous aspect of language — a "tresor depose par la pratique de la parole dans les sujets appartenant a une meme communaute") from *parole* (the individual, heterogeneous, executive act of speech). Langue is social; parole is individual. Langue is a system; parole is a set of acts.
- Location: Part I, Ch. III ("Objet de la linguistique"), approximately pp. 30-32 of the 1916 edition.
- Source of verification: The distinction is universally attested (Cambridge Companion to Saussure, Wikipedia, all secondary sources). The exact French phrasing "tresor depose par la pratique de la parole" is widely cited but the page number needs verification against the Payot 1916 edition.

### Quote 3 — The arbitrary nature of the sign [VERIFIED]
- Concept: The relationship between signifier (*signifiant*) and signified (*signifie*) is arbitrary: there is no intrinsic connection between the sound-image and the concept it denotes.
- Location: Part I, Ch. I ("Nature of the Linguistic Sign"), pp. 97-103 of the 1916 edition.
- Source of verification: Universally confirmed. This principle, combined with the langue/parole distinction, establishes that meaning is a social convention — not a natural given — and therefore culturally variable.

## 4. SAPIR-WHORF — Linguistic relativity

### 4a. SAPIR — "The Status of Linguistics as a Science" (1929)

**Bibliographic reference**: SAPIR, E., "The Status of Linguistics as a Science," *Language*, 5(4), December 1929, pp. 207-214. Reprinted in: D.G. MANDELBAUM (ed.), *Selected Writings of Edward Sapir in Language, Culture and Personality*, University of California Press, Berkeley 1949.

**Relevance to §1.2**: Sapir provides the explicit formulation of linguistic relativity that connects Humboldt's *Weltansicht* to modern cognitive linguistics. If "the real world is to a large extent unconsciously built up on the language habits of the group," then different legal languages may encode different conceptual structures — which is precisely what embedding models can make visible.

### Quote 1 — Human beings and the real world [VERIFIED]
- Original: "Human beings do not live in the objective world alone, nor alone in the world of social activity as ordinarily understood, but are very much at the mercy of the particular language which has become the medium of expression for their society."
- Location: *Language*, 5(4), 1929, p. 209.
- Source of verification: Full text available at bible-researcher.com/sapir1.html, University of Bern PDF (morrissey.unibe.ch), Max Planck PDF (pure.mpg.de). Confirmed verbatim across all three sources.

### Quote 2 — The real world unconsciously built [VERIFIED]
- Original: "The fact of the matter is that the 'real world' is to a large extent unconsciously built up on the language habits of the group."
- Location: *Language*, 5(4), 1929, p. 209 (immediately following Quote 1).
- Source of verification: Same sources as above; widely cited in secondary literature on Sapir-Whorf hypothesis.

### 4b. WHORF — "Science and Linguistics" (1940)

**Bibliographic reference**: WHORF, B.L., "Science and Linguistics," *Technology Review*, 42(6), April 1940, pp. 229-231, 247-248. Reprinted in: J.B. CARROLL (ed.), *Language, Thought, and Reality: Selected Writings of Benjamin Lee Whorf*, MIT Press, Cambridge MA 1956.

### Quote 3 — We dissect nature along linguistic lines [VERIFIED]
- Original: "We dissect nature along lines laid down by our native languages. The categories and types that we isolate from the world of phenomena we do not find there because they stare every observer in the face; on the contrary, the world is presented in a kaleidoscopic flux of impressions which has to be organized by our minds — and this means largely by the linguistic systems in our minds."
- Location: *Technology Review*, 42(6), 1940, p. 231.
- Source of verification: Full text available at MIT (web.mit.edu/allanmc/www/whorf.scienceandlinguistics.pdf). Confirmed verbatim. Also verified in Wikipedia ("Benjamin Lee Whorf") and multiple academic sources.
