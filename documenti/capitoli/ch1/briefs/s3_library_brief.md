## 1. FIRTH (1957) — "A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory, 1930-1955"

**PDF**: `firth1957_synopsis_linguistic_theory.pdf`
**Full ref**: FIRTH, J. R., "A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory, 1930-1955," in *Studies in Linguistic Analysis*, Basil Blackwell, Oxford 1957 (reprinted 1962), pp. 1-32.

### Quote 1 — Meaning as contextual relation (p.6) [VERIFIED]
> "Each function will be defined as the use of some language form or element in relation to some context. Meaning, that is to say, is to be regarded as a complex of contextual relations, and phonetics, grammar, lexicography, and semantics each handles its own components of the complex in its appropriate context."

**Relevance**: Firth's definition of meaning as "complex of contextual relations" is the direct bridge from Wittgenstein's "meaning is use" to distributional semantics. Meaning is not referential but relational.

### Quote 2 — Words as observable acts (p.2) [VERIFIED]
> "By regarding words as acts, events, habits, we limit our inquiry to what is objective and observable in the group life of our fellows."

**Relevance**: Anti-mentalist stance. Meaning is not a private mental event but an observable social phenomenon. This echoes Wittgenstein's anti-private-language argument and grounds the empirical programme.

### Quote 3 — "You shall know a word by the company it keeps" (p.11) [VERIFIED]
> "As Wittgenstein says, 'the meaning of words lies in their use.' The day to day practice of playing language games recognizes customs and rules. It follows that a text in such established usage may contain sentences such as 'Don't be such an ass!', 'You silly ass!', 'What an ass he is!' In these examples, the word *ass* is in familiar and habitual company, commonly collocated with *you silly—*, *he is a silly—*, *don't be such an—*. You shall know a word by the company it keeps!"

**Relevance**: THE key quote. Firth explicitly links Wittgenstein to distributional context. The "company it keeps" formulation is the colloquial statement of the distributional hypothesis. Note: Firth CITES Wittgenstein directly (fn 4: "See L. Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations*, pp. 7-8").

### Quote 4 — Collocation as statement of meaning (p.12) [VERIFIED]
> "Collocations of a given word are statements of the habitual or customary places of that word in collocational order and emphatically not in any other contextual order. The collocation of a word or a 'piece' is not to be regarded as mere juxtaposition, it is an order of *mutual expectancy*. The words are mutually expectant and mutually prehended."

**Relevance**: "Mutual expectancy" anticipates the statistical notion of co-occurrence probability that underlies all distributional models.

### Quote 5 — Firth cites Wittgenstein (fn on p.11) [VERIFIED]
Footnote 4 on p.11: "See L. Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations*, Oxford, Blackwell, 1953, pp. 53, 61, 80, 81."

**Relevance**: Documents that Firth was reading Wittgenstein. The Wittgenstein-Firth connection is not retrospective reconstruction; it is explicitly attested.

## 2. HARRIS (1954) — "Distributional Structure"

**PDF**: `harris1954_distributional_structure.pdf`
**Full ref**: HARRIS, Z. S., "Distributional Structure," *Word*, 10(2-3), 1954, pp. 146-162. Reprinted in J. A. FODOR and J. J. KATZ (eds.), *The Structure of Language*, Prentice-Hall, Englewood Cliffs NJ 1964, pp. 33-49.

### Quote 1 — The distributional structure thesis (p.146) [VERIFIED]
> "Does language have a distributional structure? [...] each language can be described in terms of a distributional structure, i.e. in terms of the occurrence of parts (ultimately sounds) relative to other parts, and how this description is complete without intrusion of other features such as history or meaning."

**Relevance**: Harris's programme: describe language purely in terms of distributional facts. The claim that the description is "complete without intrusion of... meaning" is the strong methodological claim that Sahlgren (2008) later nuances.

### Quote 2 — Distribution defined (p.146) [VERIFIED]
> "The distribution of an element will be understood as the sum of all its environments. An environment of an element A is an existing array of its co-occurrents, i.e. the other elements, each in a particular position, with which A occurs to yield an utterance."

**Relevance**: Technical definition of distribution. This is the formal ancestor of "context vector" in distributional semantics.

### Quote 3 — Reality of distributional structure (p.149) [VERIFIED]
> "the scientific structure states a network of relations, and these relations really hold in the data investigated"

**Relevance**: Harris argues that distributional structure is not a mere mathematical artifact but captures real patterns in the data. This is directly relevant to the thesis's claim that embedding spaces capture real semantic structure.

### Quote 4 — Is there a parallel meaning structure? (p.151) [VERIFIED]
> "The correlation between language and meaning is much greater when we consider connected discourse. To the extent that formal (distributional) structure can be discovered in discourse, it correlates in some way with the substance of what is being said."

**Relevance**: Harris acknowledges the correlation between distributional structure and meaning in discourse. This is the cautious version of the distributional hypothesis.

### Quote 5 — Meaning as a function of distribution (p.155) [VERIFIED]
> "meaning is of course a determinant in these and in other choices that we make when we speak. But as we make these choices we build a stock of utterances each of which is a particular combination of particular elements. And this stock of combinations of elements becomes a factor in the way later choices are made"

**Relevance**: Meaning influences distributional patterns, and distributional patterns in turn constrain meaning. This circularity is not vicious; it is constitutive. It anticipates the training dynamics of neural language models.

### Quote 6 — Difference of meaning correlates with difference of distribution (via Sahlgren) [VERIFIED in Sahlgren p.36]
Harris (1970: 786): "if we consider words or morphemes A and B to be more different in meaning in A and C, then we will often find that the distributions of A and B are more different than the distributions of A and C. In other words, difference of meaning correlates with difference of distribution."

**Relevance**: THE core statement of the distributional hypothesis in its most precise form. Quoted in Sahlgren (2008, p.36) from Harris's *Mathematical Structures of Language* (1968/1970).

## 3. SAHLGREN (2008) — "The distributional hypothesis"

**PDF**: `sahlgren2008_distributional_hypothesis.pdf`
**Full ref**: SAHLGREN, M., "The distributional hypothesis," *Rivista di Linguistica*, 20(1), 2008, pp. 33-53.

### Quote 1 — Standard formulations (p.33) [VERIFIED]
> "This hypothesis is often stated in terms like 'words which are similar in meaning occur in similar contexts' (Rubenstein & Goodenough 1965); 'words with similar meanings will occur with similar neighbors if enough text material is available' (Schütze & Pedersen 1995); 'a representation that captures much of how words are used in natural context will capture much of what we mean by meaning' (Landauer & Dumais 1997)"

**Relevance**: Collects the canonical formulations of the distributional hypothesis from different traditions.

### Quote 2 — Structuralist roots in Saussure (p.38) [VERIFIED]
> "the origin of the differential view on meaning goes back even further, to the cradle of structuralism and the *Cours de linguistique générale* (1916/1983). It is in this work Ferdinand de Saussure lays the foundation for what will later develop into structuralism."
> "In the language itself, there are only differences." (Saussure 1916/1983: 166-118)

**Relevance**: Sahlgren traces the DH back through Bloomfield and Harris to Saussure. The differential view of meaning (signs defined by difference from other signs, not by reference) is the deep theoretical foundation.

### Quote 3 — Saussure's *valeur* and *mouton/sheep* (p.38) [VERIFIED]
> "The concept of *valeur* corresponds to the idea of a thoroughly linguistic aspect of meaning. Consider the difference between the French word *mouton* and the English word *sheep*. These words may be said to have the same extralinguistic (i.e. referential) meaning, but they do not have the same valeur, since English makes a distinction between *mutton* and *sheep* that is not available in French."

**Relevance**: Saussure's *valeur* = meaning as position within a system of differences. This is exactly what embedding spaces capture: a word's meaning is its position relative to all other words.

### Quote 4 — Syntagmatic vs paradigmatic relations (p.39) [VERIFIED]
> "SYNTAGMATIC RELATIONS concern positioning, and relate entities that co-occur in the text; it is a relation *in praesentia*. [...] PARADIGMATIC RELATIONS concern substitution, and relate entities that do not co-occur in the text; it is a relation *in absentia*."

**Relevance**: The two axes of linguistic structure. Co-occurrence models (word2vec skip-gram, LSA) capture syntagmatic relations; shared-neighbor models capture paradigmatic relations.

### Quote 5 — The refined distributional hypothesis (p.40) [VERIFIED]
> "THE REFINED DISTRIBUTIONAL HYPOTHESIS: A distributional model accumulated from co-occurrence information contains syntagmatic relations between words, while a distributional model accumulated from information about shared neighbors contains paradigmatic relations between words."

**Relevance**: Sahlgren's key contribution. Specifies *what kind* of semantic information different distributional models capture. Important for §2.3 (model selection) but introduced here as theoretical refinement.

### Quote 6 — Harris's differential view of meaning (p.36) [VERIFIED]
> "What Harris is saying here is that even if extralinguistic factors *do* influence linguistic events, there will always be a distributional correlate to the event that will suffice as explanatory principle. [...] whatever is *internal* to language is subject to linguistic analysis, which for Harris meant *distributional* analysis."

**Relevance**: Harris's methodological commitment: meaning, insofar as it is linguistic, is distributional. This grounds the thesis's claim that embedding spaces (which capture distributional structure) capture linguistic meaning.

## 4. OSGOOD, SUCI & TANNENBAUM (1957) — *The Measurement of Meaning*

**PDF**: `osgood1957_measurement_of_meaning.pdf`
**Full ref**: OSGOOD, C. E., SUCI, G. J., and TANNENBAUM, P. H., *The Measurement of Meaning*, University of Illinois Press, Urbana 1957.

### Quote 1 — The programme (p.1) [VERIFIED]
> "Apart from the studies to be reported here, there have been few, if any, systematic attempts to subject meaning to quantitative measurement."

**Relevance**: THE opening sentence. Osgood identifies the same gap that §1.1 describes: meaning is not measured. His book is the first systematic attempt.

### Quote 2 — Linguistic meaning as distributional (p.3) [VERIFIED]
> "the 'meaning' of a linguistic unit (phoneme, morpheme, etc.) [is] its function or position in the code system as a whole. Thus the 'linguistic meaning' of *the happy boy* in the utterance *the happy boy is playing in the pond* is 'nominative substantive form class' [...] It is clear that these meanings of 'meaning' serve to define the relationship of signs to other signs in the message matrix"

**Relevance**: Osgood summarises the distributional-linguistic definition of meaning (citing Joos 1950, Harris 1951). Meaning = position in a system of relations. This is the linguistic precursor to the geometric intuition.

### Quote 3 — The semantic differential method (Preface + Ch.1-2 titles) [VERIFIED]
Preface: "a group of us at the University of Illinois has been concentrating on the development of an objective measure of *meaning*"
Ch.2 title: "THE DIMENSIONALITY OF THE SEMANTIC SPACE"
Ch.3 title: "THE SEMANTIC DIFFERENTIAL AS A MEASURING INSTRUMENT"

**Relevance**: The semantic differential places concepts in a *space* defined by bipolar scales (good-bad, strong-weak, active-passive). Factor analysis yields three dimensions: Evaluation, Potency, Activity (EPA). This is the *first spatial model of meaning*. The geometric intuition (meaning = position in a space) is present; the computational power to realize it at scale is not.

## 5. KOZLOWSKI, TADDY & EVANS (2019) — "The Geometry of Culture"

**PDF**: `arxiv_1803.09288_geometry_of_culture_kozlowski2019.pdf`
**Full ref**: KOZLOWSKI, A. C., TADDY, M., and EVANS, J. A., "The Geometry of Culture: Analyzing Meaning through Word Embeddings," *American Sociological Review*, 84(5), 2019, pp. 905-949.

### Quote 1 — Abstract: relational model of meaning (p.1) [VERIFIED]
> "Word embeddings represent semantic relations between words as geometric relationships between vectors in a high-dimensional space, operationalizing a relational model of meaning consistent with contemporary theories of identity and culture."

**Relevance**: "Relational model of meaning" is exactly the thesis's framing. Kozlowski shows that embedding geometry captures cultural structure, not just linguistic similarity.

### Quote 2 — Dimensions as cultural dimensions (p.1) [VERIFIED]
> "We show that dimensions induced by word differences (e.g., *man - woman*, *rich - poor*, *black - white*, *liberal - conservative*) in these vector spaces closely correspond to dimensions of cultural meaning"

**Relevance**: Cultural categories are geometrically encoded as directions in embedding space. The method (axis projection) is used in the thesis's Lens IV (value axes).

### Quote 3 — Cross-cultural comparison via embeddings (p.4) [VERIFIED]
> "By comparing word embedding models trained on texts produced in different cultural contexts, it is possible to directly examine differences in the meanings of terms and categories between social groups."

**Relevance**: Direct precedent for the thesis's cross-tradition comparison. Kozlowski compares US and UK; the thesis compares WEIRD and Sinic legal traditions.

### Quote 4 — High-dimensional theorizing (p.1) [VERIFIED]
> "the success of these high-dimensional models motivates a move towards 'high-dimensional theorizing' of meanings, identities and cultural processes."

**Relevance**: The call for "high-dimensional theorizing" is the sociological parallel to the thesis's epistemological argument: complex cultural phenomena require high-dimensional instruments.

## 6. CALISKAN, BRYSON & NARAYANAN (2017) — "Semantics derived automatically from language corpora contain human-like biases"

**PDF**: `caliskan2017_semantics_biases_science.pdf`
**Full ref**: CALISKAN, A., BRYSON, J. J., and NARAYANAN, A., "Semantics derived automatically from language corpora contain human-like biases," *Science*, 356(6334), 2017, pp. 183-186.

### Quote 1 — Abstract (p.1 of author version) [VERIFIED]
> "Machine learning is a means to derive artificial intelligence by discovering patterns in existing data. Here we show that applying machine learning to ordinary human language results in human-like semantic biases."
> "Our results indicate that text corpora contain recoverable and accurate imprints of our historic biases, whether morally neutral as towards insects or flowers, problematic as towards race or gender, or even simply veridical, reflecting the *status quo* distribution of gender with respect to careers or first names."

**Relevance**: The *Science* paper that established embeddings as cultural mirrors. "Recoverable and accurate imprints" is the key formulation: the model is a measurement instrument for the semantic practices of its training community.

### Quote 2 — No direct experience of the world (p.3) [VERIFIED]
> "Notice that the word embeddings 'know' these properties of flowers, insects, musical instruments, and weapons with no direct experience of the world, and no representation of semantics other than the implicit metrics of words' co-occurrence statistics with other nearby words."

**Relevance**: The model has no referential access to the world. All its "knowledge" is distributional. This confirms the Wittgensteinian premise: meaning is constituted by patterns of use, not by reference to objects.

## 7. GARG, SCHIEBINGER, JURAFSKY & ZOU (2018) — "Word Embeddings Quantify 100 Years of Gender and Ethnic Stereotypes"

**PDF**: `pnas_garg2018_word_embeddings_stereotypes.pdf`
**Full ref**: GARG, N., SCHIEBINGER, L., JURAFSKY, D., and ZOU, J., "Word Embeddings Quantify 100 Years of Gender and Ethnic Stereotypes," *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 115(16), 2018, pp. E3635-E3644.

### Quote 1 — Embeddings as quantitative lens (p.2) [VERIFIED]
> "Our results demonstrate that word embeddings are a powerful lens through which we can systematically quantify common stereotypes and other historical trends. Embeddings thus provide an important new quantitative metric which complements existing (more qualitative) linguistic and sociological analyses of biases."

**Relevance**: "Quantitative metric which complements existing qualitative analyses" is exactly the thesis's epistemological position: embedding spaces do not replace hermeneutic interpretation but supplement it with measurable structure.

### Quote 2 — External validation against Census (p.2-3) [VERIFIED]
> "We validate our findings on external metrics and show that our results are robust to the different algorithms for training the word embeddings."
> "the embedding bias accurately captures both gender and ethic occupation proportion and consistently reflects historical changes" (p.4)

**Relevance**: Garg validates embedding measurements against independent data (US Census). This is critical for the thesis's argument that embedding spaces are measurement instruments, not just statistical curiosities. External validation = measurement validity.

### Quote 3 — Diachronic framework (p.1) [VERIFIED]
> "we develop a framework to demonstrate how the temporal dynamics of the embedding can be leveraged to quantify changes in stereotypes and attitudes toward women and ethnic minorities in the 20th and 21st centuries in the United States"

**Relevance**: Demonstrates that embedding spaces can track *change* over time. Not directly used in the thesis (which is synchronic), but important as precedent for the claim that embeddings capture real cultural phenomena.

## 8. MIKOLOV, CHEN, CORRADO & DEAN (2013) — "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space"

**PDF**: `arxiv_1301.3781_word2vec_mikolov2013.pdf`
**Full ref**: MIKOLOV, T., CHEN, K., CORRADO, G., and DEAN, J., "Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space," *Proceedings of ICLR 2013 Workshop*, 2013.

### Quote 1 — The King-Queen analogy (p.2) [VERIFIED]
> "Somewhat surprisingly, it was found that similarity of word representations goes beyond simple syntactic regularities. Using a word offset technique where simple algebraic operations are performed on the word vectors, it was shown for example that *vector("King") - vector("Man") + vector("Woman")* results in a vector that is closest to the vector representation of the word *Queen*."

**Relevance**: THE iconic result. Demonstrates that semantic *relations* (not just similarities) are geometrically encoded as vector operations. This is the computational proof-of-concept for the distributional hypothesis: meaning structure is algebraic.

### Quote 2 — CBOW and Skip-gram (pp.4-5) [VERIFIED]
Two architectures: CBOW (predict word from context) and Skip-gram (predict context from word). Both are implementations of the distributional hypothesis: learn word representations from co-occurrence patterns.

**Relevance**: Technical detail for §2.3, but introduced here conceptually: these are the architectures that made the distributional hypothesis computationally tractable at scale.
