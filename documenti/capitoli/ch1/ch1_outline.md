# Chapter 1 — Meaning without measure
## Ius sine regula

**Thesis chapter**: Ch.1
**Status**: outline — ready for drafting
**Date**: 2026-03-07

---

## Disciplinary frame

This chapter belongs to **methodology of legal science**, not comparative law.
The central question is epistemological: *can legal meaning be measured, and if
so, what kind of knowledge does such measurement produce?*

The cross-tradition comparison (WEIRD vs Sinic) appears only in §1.4 as the
**experimental design** that validates the measurement instrument. Comparative
law is the first beneficiary of the instrument, not the discipline of the thesis.

---

## Argumentative arc

The chapter constructs a bridge between three traditions that have never
directly spoken to each other:

| Tradition | Key insight | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Legal philosophy (Savigny, Ehrlich) | Legal meaning emerges from collective practice, not from the legislator's will | No instrument to *measure* this practice |
| Philosophy of language (Wittgenstein, Firth, Harris) | Meaning IS use; distributional context defines sense | Never applied to the legal domain as measurement |
| Computational neuroscience (Shepard, Kriegeskorte) | Two spaces are comparable by comparing *relations between points*, not points themselves (second-order isomorphism) | Never applied to legal semantic spaces |

**The argument**: if meaning is practice (Savigny/Wittgenstein), and if practice
can be captured computationally (distributional hypothesis), and if the resulting
spaces can be compared via second-order isomorphism (Kriegeskorte), then legal
meaning is *measurable* — not as propositional content, but as relational
geometric structure.

**The measurability thesis**: the relational organization of legal concepts in
embedding spaces constitutes an observable, measurable, and empirically testable
object of study for legal science.

---

## Relationship to the introduction

The introduction is a summary of the entire thesis (apparatus, experiments,
results). Chapter 1 develops only the theoretical-epistemological argument
that makes the inquiry intelligible. No experiment details, no results — only
the path from "law has no measurement instrument" to "here is why one can
now be constructed."

## Relationship to DigiVolksgeist (Puzio 2025)

The DigiVolksgeist paper argued that LLMs *crystallise* the Volksgeist
(ontological claim). Chapter 1 argues that embedding spaces can serve as
*instruments of measurement* for legal meaning (epistemological claim). Same
intellectual tradition (Savigny → Wittgenstein → Ehrlich), different epistemic
ambition. §1.2 draws on the same sources but reframes them toward measurability,
not crystallisation. Must be reworked, not copied.

## Section outline

### §1.1 A science that does not measure: the epistemological gap
**Target**: ~1500 words
**Thesis section ref**: → §1.1

#### 1.1.a The paradox
- Every fundamental legal operation (subsumption, analogy, qualification)
  presupposes determinate semantic content
- Meaning is assessed, debated, adjudicated — never measured
- This is not a problem of comparative law alone: it concerns every jurist
  who performs subsumption, every court that distinguishes cases

#### 1.1.b The hermeneutic tradition: principled rejection, not mere absence
- Kelsen, *Reine Rechtslehre* (1960): validity ≠ observable fact; the
  Sein/Sollen separation as foundation of pure legal science
- Gadamer, *Wahrheit und Methode* (1960): understanding as participation in
  a tradition, not as measurement of an object
- Betti, *Teoria generale della interpretazione* (1955): interpretation as
  recognition of meaning, not quantification
- Savigny's four canons (grammatical, logical, historical, systematic) as
  qualitative methods of semantic access
- Bobbio, *Teoria della scienza del diritto* (1950): the epistemological
  status of legal science — is it a science at all? [relevant for LUISS]
- **Key point**: the tradition has *reasons*, not just habits, for rejecting
  measurement. These reasons must be taken seriously before proposing an
  alternative.

#### 1.1.c The contrast with natural science
- Galileo's telescope: did not replace reasoning, gave it new objects —
  objects that could be shared, reproduced, and contested on the basis of
  evidence rather than authority alone
- The epistemological asymmetry: the jurist's relationship to meaning
  remains pre-instrumental
- Legal NLP (case prediction, contract classification, statutory retrieval)
  *consumes* meaning but does not *examine* it. The question is instrumental
  ("can a model perform a legal task?"), not epistemological ("does legal
  meaning have observable structure?")
- Lettieri (2018), Chau & Livermore (2024): the programme of computational
  legal science — ambitious but still task-oriented

#### 1.1.d The question
- Is the absence of measurement a necessary feature of legal knowledge or
  a historical contingency?
- **Transition**: the tradition that most clearly links law to
  meaning-as-practice paradoxically opens the path to measurement.

### §1.2 Law as language: Volksgeist, lebendes Recht, and meaning as collective practice
**Target**: ~2500 words
**Thesis section ref**: → §1.2

#### 1.2.a Savigny: law is found, not made
- The Thibaut-Savigny debate (1814) as background: codification vs organic
  emergence
- *Vom Beruf unsrer Zeit* (1814): law arises from the collective consciousness
  of the people (*Rechtsbewußtseyn*)
- *System des heutigen römischen Rechts* (1840, §7): «Das Recht hat seine
  Wurzel in dem gemeinsamen Bewußtseyn des Volkes»
- The constitutive analogy with language: no one invents a grammar; no one
  decrees the rules of syntax. Grammar exists, binds, is transmitted.
  Law is the same.
- **Methodological reading** (not comparativist): Savigny tells us that legal
  meaning is a *collective emergent phenomenon*. The question for methodology
  is: can such phenomena be observed empirically?

#### 1.2.b Language as Weltansicht: Herder, Humboldt
- Herder (1772): language as the "natural organ" of thought; each people's
  spirit expressed through its language
- Humboldt (1836): every language is a *Weltansicht*; «die Sprache ist das
  bildende Organ des Gedankens»
- Brief mention of Sapir-Whorf (linguistic relativity) as the modern
  descendant — controversial in its strong form, but the weak form (language
  influences conceptual organization) is well established
- **Implication for legal methodology**: if law is analogous to language
  (Savigny), and if language shapes conceptual structure (Humboldt), then
  every legal tradition organizes its normative concepts according to the
  categories of its linguistic community. This is not a comparative claim —
  it is a claim about how legal meaning is constituted.

#### 1.2.c Wittgenstein: meaning is use
- *Philosophische Untersuchungen* (1953):
  - §43: the meaning of a word is its use in the language
  - §19: to imagine a language is to imagine a *Lebensform*
  - §199, §202: following a rule is participating in a *Gepflogenheit*
  - §241: agreement not in opinions but in form of life
- The Sprachspiel (§23): meaning arises within specific language-games,
  not in the abstract
- **The hinge of the chapter**: if meaning is use, then meaning is in
  principle *observable* — it is constituted by patterns of practice, not
  hidden in mental states. But "use" in Wittgenstein is a qualitative
  notion. The question is whether it can be formalized.

#### 1.2.d Ehrlich: living law
- *Grundlegung der Soziologie des Rechts* (1913):
  - *Entscheidungsnormen* (norms of decision) vs *lebendes Recht* (living law)
  - Living law: the norms actually governing social life, often diverging
    from formal law
- The gap between declared norm and practised norm
- **Methodological relevance**: Ehrlich's living law is the closest the
  jurisprudential tradition has come to saying "legal meaning is empirical
  practice." But Ehrlich's method remained ethnographic/sociological — he
  had no instrument for measuring semantic structure at scale.

#### 1.2.e Synthesis and transition
- This tradition provides the richest account of meaning as collective
  practice: emergent (Savigny), culturally constitutive (Humboldt),
  use-based (Wittgenstein), empirically grounded (Ehrlich)
- But its methodology remains hermeneutic: interpretation of texts, not
  measurement of structures
- Wittgenstein says meaning is use. But "use" is qualitative. Is there a
  formalization?
- **Transition**: the formalization exists. It is called the distributional
  hypothesis.

**Sources for §1.2**:
- Savigny, *Vom Beruf unsrer Zeit* (1814)
- Savigny, *System des heutigen römischen Rechts* (1840, §7-8)
- Herder, *Abhandlung über den Ursprung der Sprache* (1772)
- Humboldt, *Über die Verschiedenheit des menschlichen Sprachbaues* (1836)
- Wittgenstein, *Philosophische Untersuchungen* (1953)

### §1.3 Language as space: from "meaning is use" to the distributional hypothesis
**Target**: ~2500 words
**Thesis section ref**: → §1.3

#### 1.3.a The distributional hypothesis: Firth and Harris
- Firth (1957): «You shall know a word by the company it keeps» — the
  distributional hypothesis in its most quoted form
- Harris (1954): "Distributional structure" — co-occurrence patterns as the
  empirical basis of meaning
- **The Wittgenstein-Firth connection**: rarely made explicit, but
  fundamental. "Meaning is use" → "meaning is distributional context."
  Sahlgren (2008) traces this bridge explicitly
- This is not a reductive move: it is the operationalization of
  Wittgenstein's insight. If meaning is constituted by patterns of use,
  then observing those patterns is observing meaning.

#### 1.3.b The prehistory of meaning-as-space
- Osgood, Suci & Tannenbaum (1957), *The Measurement of Meaning*: the
  semantic differential as the first spatial model of meaning
- Three dimensions (evaluation, potency, activity) — limited, but the
  geometric intuition is present: meaning can be represented as position
  in a space
- The gap between the intuition and the computational power to realize it

#### 1.3.c The computational revolution
- Mikolov et al. (2013): word2vec — the distributional hypothesis
  operationalized at scale. "king − man + woman ≈ queen" as proof that
  semantic structure is encoded geometrically
- Pennington et al. (2014): GloVe — global co-occurrence statistics
- Vaswani et al. (2017): the Transformer architecture
- Devlin et al. (2019): BERT — contextual, layer-wise representations
- Reimers & Gurevych (2019): Sentence-BERT — full sentences as vectors
- **Keep technical detail minimal**: this is a theoretical chapter. The
  architectures are described conceptually; §2.3 provides the technical
  apparatus.

#### 1.3.d Embedding spaces as objects of study
- The paradigm shift: from "using models to do tasks" to "studying models
  as objects." The model not as prediction engine but as *cultural artifact*
  whose internal structure reflects the community that produced the
  training text
- Kozlowski et al. (2019): the geometry of culture — class, gender, race
  as measurable dimensions in embedding spaces
- Garg et al. (2018): 100 years of stereotypes tracked through diachronic
  embeddings
- Caliskan, Bryson & Narayanan (2017): the *Science* paper — embeddings
  contain human-like biases. The model as a statistical proxy for the
  semantic practices of a population
- Hamilton et al. (2016): semantic change over time
- Bolukbasi et al. (2016): the debiasing programme — treating cultural
  information as defect rather than data
- **Methodological significance**: these social-science applications
  demonstrate that embedding spaces can serve as *instruments of
  measurement* for cultural phenomena. The question for legal methodology
  is whether the same is true for legal meaning.

#### 1.3.e Transition: the comparability problem
- If embedding spaces capture meaning as structure, can we treat them as
  measurement instruments for legal science?
- Problem: different models produce different spaces (different
  dimensionalities, architectures, training data). Direct vector
  comparison across models is meaningless.
- We need a framework for comparing the *structure* of spaces, not the
  spaces themselves.
- **Transition**: such a framework exists. It was developed in cognitive
  neuroscience.

### §1.4 Space as legal instrument: second-order isomorphism and the measurability thesis
**Target**: ~2000 words
**Thesis section ref**: → §1.4

#### 1.4.a The problem of cross-space comparison
- Restate concretely: a 768-dimensional vector and a 1024-dimensional vector
  live in incommensurable spaces
- The naive approach (multilingual models, translation-based alignment)
  would *impose* correspondence rather than *discover* it
- The monolingual-model design as methodological commitment: each model
  absorbs only its own linguistic community's practices (developed in §2.3)

#### 1.4.b Shepard and Chipman: second-order isomorphism
- Shepard & Chipman (1970): "Second-order isomorphism of internal
  representations: Shapes of states"
- First-order isomorphism: the representation *resembles* the object
  (a map that looks like the territory)
- Second-order isomorphism: the representation preserves *relations among
  objects* without resembling them individually. "Similarities between
  representations correspond to similarities between objects"
- This is exactly the situation with embedding models: the vectors need
  not be comparable; the *relations between vectors* can be

#### 1.4.c Kriegeskorte: Representational Similarity Analysis
- Kriegeskorte, Mur & Bandettini (2008): the operationalization of
  second-order isomorphism for neural data
- The RDM (Representational Dissimilarity Matrix): for N concepts, an N×N
  matrix of pairwise distances within a single space
- Comparing spaces = comparing RDMs (Spearman ρ on upper triangles)
- The Mantel test and block bootstrap for proper statistical inference
  (respecting dependence structure)
- **Conceptual framework only here**: the statistical details are in §2.4

#### 1.4.d The measurability thesis
- If legal concepts occupy positions in embedding spaces (§1.3)
- And if the relational structure between these positions is stable and
  non-random (empirical question, tested in §3.1)
- And if relational structures can be compared across independently
  constructed spaces via second-order isomorphism (§1.4.b-c)
- Then: **the relational organization of legal concepts in embedding spaces
  constitutes an observable, measurable, and empirically testable object
  of study for legal science**
- This is the measurability thesis. It does not claim that all of legal
  meaning is captured — only the *relational geometric structure* of
  meaning: which concepts are near, which are far, which domains are
  cohesive, which are fragmented.
- Distinguish: measurable ≠ exhaustive. We measure the geometry, not the
  hermeneutics.

#### 1.4.e What becomes possible: instrument validation logic
- With the measurability thesis, three questions become empirically
  addressable — framed as **instrument validation**, not comparative law:
  1. **Does the signal exist?** Does legal meaning have non-random geometric
     structure in embedding spaces? (§3.1 — domain signal, topology)
  2. **Is the instrument reliable?** Is this structure stable across
     independently trained models within the same tradition?
     (§3.1.4 — within-tradition RSA)
  3. **Does the instrument have discriminating power?** Can it detect
     differences where we expect them to exist? (§3.2 neighborhoods,
     §3.3 value axes — cross-tradition as validation against known
     variation)
- The cross-tradition comparison is therefore the *validation protocol*
  for the measurement instrument. A measurement instrument is validated
  by exposing it to known variation; without variation, there is nothing
  to measure. Hong Kong provides this variation under controlled
  institutional conditions.

### §1.5 What this work does not claim
**Target**: ~1200 words
**Thesis section ref**: → §1.5

#### 1.5.a Law is not geometry
- The thesis does not reduce law to its geometric representation
- It asks whether geometry can be a legitimate *instrument* of legal
  knowledge
- The telescope analogy: the telescope did not claim celestial bodies ARE
  optics; it used optics to observe them

#### 1.5.b Embeddings do not capture all of meaning
- What embeddings capture: distributional relational structure — the
  statistical sediment of how a linguistic community uses legal concepts
- What they do not: argumentation, institutional context, legislative
  intent, normative force, precedential reasoning, the hermeneutic
  richness of interpretation
- The object of measurement is the *structure* of meaning, not meaning
  in its full juristic sense

#### 1.5.c Measurement does not replace interpretation
- The embedding space is the telescope, not the astronomer
- The instrument reveals structures; the jurist gives them legal
  significance
- The jurist completes the analysis where the number provides the measure
  but not the judgment (→ §4.1 "Quod numeri tacent")

#### 1.5.d Models are not culturally neutral
- Every model has its *Volk* (DigiVolksgeist)
- The monolingual design isolates the cultural variable; it does not
  eliminate it
- The panel of three models per tradition is a robustness control: a
  finding is attributed to the tradition, not to a single model's
  idiosyncrasies, only if it replicates across the panel

#### 1.5.e Correlation, not causation
- Geometric patterns are structural correlations, not causal explanations
- The thesis describes *what* the geometry shows, not *why* traditions
  differ at a historical or political level

#### 1.5.f This is not comparative law
- The cross-tradition comparison is the experimental design, not the thesis
- A measurement instrument requires variation to be validated; without
  variation, there is nothing to measure
- Hong Kong provides variation under controlled institutional conditions
  (same legal concepts in two linguistic traditions within a shared
  normative framework)
- The questions are methodological: *can* embedding spaces serve as
  instruments of legal knowledge? The answers may benefit comparative law,
  but they belong to the methodology of legal science

**Sources for §1.5**:
- Primarily argumentative; draws on the introduction's closing paragraph
- Forward references to §4.1 (what the numbers say together) and §4.2
  (model-data-culture entanglement)
