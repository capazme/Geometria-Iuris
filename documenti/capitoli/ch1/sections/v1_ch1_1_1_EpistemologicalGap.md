# Chapter 1 — Meaning without measure

## 1.1 A science that does not measure: the epistemological gap in legal knowledge


Every fundamental operation of legal reasoning is an operation on meaning. When a court subsumes a particular set of facts under a general norm, it performs a semantic act: it determines that the factual situation falls within the class of cases designated by the rule. When a jurist reasons by analogy, he identifies a structural similarity between two constellations of meaning. Whenever a legislator qualifies conduct as lawful or unlawful, he draws a boundary through a conceptual space. Subsumption, analogy, qualification, distinction: each of these operations presupposes that legal concepts carry determinate semantic content, content that can be weighed, compared, and contested. Meaning, in law, is not a marginal concern.[^1]

Yet legal science possesses no instrument for measuring what it constantly manipulates. Meaning is assessed through argument, debated in courtrooms, adjudicated by supreme courts. It is never measured. No device renders the distance between two legal concepts visible; no procedure makes the boundaries of a normative category intersubjectively observable; no method allows one jurist to reproduce another’s semantic judgment and test it against shared evidence.

This absence is not an accident. It reflects a disciplinary tradition that has, on principled grounds, rejected measurement as a mode of access to legal meaning.


This rejection is nowhere more explicit than in Kelsen’s Reine Rechtslehre, which established its foundational axiom precisely by severing the normative from the empirical. The Sein/Sollenpure legal science. Kelsen conceived his project in explicit analogy with Kant’s critical philosophy: just as Kant asked how a metaphysics-free account of natural phenomena is possible, «In the same way, the Pure Theory of Law asks: “How is it possible to interpret without recourse to meta-legal authorities, like God or nature, the subjective meaning of certain facts as a system of objectively valid legal norms describable in rules of law”». The Grundnorm, the apex of the normative pyramid, is not an empirical proposition but what Kelsen himself called «die transzendental-logische Bedingung dieser Deutung» (Lit. «the transcendental-logical condition of this interpretation»). Within this framework, the idea that meaning could be observed rather than interpreted is not merely unorthodox; it is a category error.[^2][^3][^4][^5]

Gadamer’s Wahrheit und Methode, published in the same year as the second edition of the Reine Rechtslehre, mounted a different but equally consequential resistance. For Gadamer, understanding is not a technique applied to an alien object but a mode of participation in the tradition that constitutes both interpreter and text. The interpreter does not stand outside the text with a measuring instrument; he is situated within an hermeneutical situation shaped by what Gadamer called “wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewusstsein” (de. “consciousness of being affected by history”), so the awareness that understanding is always already conditioned by the history of the object’s effects upon the interpreter. Prejudice, far from being an obstacle to be eliminated, is the condition of understanding: «the overcoming of all prejudices, this global demand of the Enlightenment, will itself prove to be a prejudice». The anticipation of meaning that governs interpretation is not an act of subjectivity but proceeds, in Gadamer’s account, «from the commonality that binds us to the tradition.»[^6][^7][^8][^9]

Betti’s Teoria generale della interpretazione (it. General Theory of Interpretation, 1955) offered a more methodologically explicit counterpart. Against what he perceived as the subjectivism latent in Gadamer’s approach, Betti insisted that interpretation is “riconoscimento di senso” (recognition of meaning), not “Sinngebung” (attribution of meaning). His four canons of interpretation, divided between requirements ex parte objecti and ex parte subjecti, constitute a systematic attempt to ensure objectivity without recourse to quantification: the canon of hermeneutic autonomy demands representative forms to be understood «secondo quello spirito che si è obiettivato in esse» (it. «according to the spirit objectified in them»); the canon of totality requires constant attention to the correlation «tra le parti e il tutto di cui fanno parte» (it. «between the parts and the whole of which they are part»); the canon of actuality acknowledges the interpreter’s present perspective while prohibiting anachronism; the canon of adequacy calls for congeniality between interpreter and object. These would be rigorous constraints, but constraints on judgment, not protocols of measurement. They presuppose a subject capable of recognition, not an instrument capable of detection.[^10][^11]

The same qualitative orientation pervades the long-standing account of legal method. Savigny’s four elements of interpretation, systematized by Larenz in his Methodenlehre der Rechtswissenschaft, define the jurist’s toolkit: das grammatische Element (the meaning of words in their linguistic context), das logische Element (the internal coherence of the proposition), das historische Element (the circumstances of the norm’s genesis), and das systematische Element (the norm’s place within the legal order). Each element is a mode of hermeneutic access to meaning. None produces a measurement. Larenz himself, in introducing his methodological programme, acknowledged the point explicitly, stating that «one must free oneself from the notion that the results obtained in this way could attain the same degree of certainty and exactitude as a mathematical proof or an exactly executed measurement». In these terms, subsumption is an act of logical judgment guided by practical reason, not an empirical and objective operation. The jurist determines whether a case falls within a category, he does not measure the distance between the case and the category’s boundary.[^12][^13]

Bobbio crystallized the epistemological implication of this tradition. Legal science, he argued, is a discourse upon a discourse, a descriptive meta-language of the prescriptive language of the legislator. If law is prescription and legal science is the description of prescription, then legal science occupies a peculiar epistemic position: it is a discipline whose object is itself normative, making it fundamentally unlike the natural sciences, which describe empirical regularities. This double subtraction, from facts to norms to the description of norms, makes the idea of measurement seem doubly foreign. What would one be measuring? Not facts (that is the domain of sociology). Not norms (they are prescriptions, not observables). But the structure of the metalanguage itself?[^14][^15]

The comparison with natural science sharpens the point. When Galileo directed his telescope toward Jupiter in January 1610, the instrument did not replace the astronomer’s reasoning. What it did was more fundamental: it rendered the object of inquiry intersubjectively accessible. The four moons became data in a precise sense, objects available for independent reproduction and contestation grounded in observation rather than in the prestige of the observer. One could disagree about the significance of the moons, but one could not deny their existence without refusing to look through the lens. The instrument transformed the kind of dispute that was possible: from disputes about “what authority said” to disputes about “what the evidence showed”.[^16]


The emergence of computational approaches to law has not, so far, altered this condition. The field variously known as computational legal studies, legal Natural Language Processing (NLP), or legal informatics has produced substantial work: sentiment analysis of judicial opinions, topic modelling of legislation, prediction of case outcomes, classification of contracts. Two paradigms have taken shape: law-as-code, which seeks to represent legal rules in executable format, and law-as-data, which leverages quantitative analysis of legal texts to determine «the content, causes, or consequences of legal decisions». Word embeddings, in particular, have entered legal scholarship as tools for supervised learning and text analysis, capturing «the semantic meaning of words by mapping them to vectors of real numbers.» These are genuine advances. But they share a common orientation: they are instrumental. They ask whether a model can perform a legal task, whether an algorithm can predict a judicial decision, whether a classifier can distinguish relevant from irrelevant documents. They[^17][^18][^19]

The distinction is consequential. Bhupatiraju, Chen, and Venkataramanan have observed that the formal-theoretical literature has long treated law spatially, with case-space models that separate the fact space into regions of liability and non-liability; yet, they note, «it has been infeasible to measure the legal case space». Legal scholarship has conceived of law geometrically without possessing the instruments to measure the geometry. Meanwhile, NLP research has provided the instruments without asking what they reveal about the structure of meaning. Lettieri and colleagues have rightly identified data and computation as “epistemic enhancers”, artifacts that extend observational and cognitive abilities. But they have also noted that, compared to other sciences, «the legal world has accumulated some delay» in exploiting this potential.measure when they are applied to legal language has scarcely been posed. Computation has provided law with better tools for performing legal tasks, but it has not yet provided legal science with better instruments for observing its own object.[^20][^21][^22]

Is the absence of measurement a necessary feature of legal knowledge, or a historical contingency? Kelsen’s Sein/Sollen separation, Gadamer’s hermeneutic ontology, Betti’s canons of recognition: each offers powerful reasons for regarding legal meaning as resistant to empirical measurement.


The tradition that  this observation is, perhaps unexpectedly, the same one that has most firmly situated legal meaning in collective practice: the line running from Savigny, for whom law was rooted in the common consciousness of the people, through Wittgenstein, for whom the meaning of a word was its use in the language, to Ehrlich, for whom living law was the law actually practiced rather than the law merely declared. If meaning is constituted by patterns of use, as this tradition maintains, then the question is whether those patterns can be captured with sufficient fidelity and at sufficient scale to serve as the basis for measurement.
